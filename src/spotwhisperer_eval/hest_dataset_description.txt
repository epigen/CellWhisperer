The HEST1K spatial transcriptomics dataset represents a comprehensive collection of 1,229 samples spanning both healthy and diseased tissues across multiple organ systems. The dataset is heavily weighted toward neurological tissues, with spinal cord (318 samples) and brain (211 samples) comprising nearly half of all samples, suggesting a strong focus on central nervous system research. Cancer represents a major disease category with 367 samples, complemented by an additional 453 samples classified as "diseased" and 7 specifically labeled as "tumor," indicating substantial representation of oncological conditions. The oncology focus is further evidenced by specific cancer types including invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC, 125 samples), colorectal adenocarcinoma (COAD, 58 samples), clear cell renal cell carcinoma (SCCRCC, 24 samples), and various other malignancies affecting organs such as liver, prostate, pancreas, and skin.

The tissue representation spans major organ systems with particular emphasis on breast (125 samples), gastrointestinal tract (bowel: 94 samples, colon: 62 samples), skin (88 samples), cardiovascular system (heart: 70 samples), and genitourinary organs (prostate: 62 samples, kidney: 46 samples). The dataset includes both human (649 samples) and mouse (580 samples) specimens, enabling comparative studies across species. Notably, the dataset contains 345 healthy samples alongside diseased tissues, providing crucial control comparisons for understanding disease-associated spatial gene expression patterns.

This dataset appears particularly valuable for investigating spatial transcriptomic signatures in cancer biology, neurological disorders, and normal tissue architecture across diverse organ systems. The substantial representation of central nervous system tissues suggests potential applications in studying neurodegenerative diseases, brain tumors, and spinal cord pathologies. The inclusion of treated samples (53) and genetically modified specimens (4) adds dimensions for studying therapeutic responses and experimental disease models, making this a versatile resource for spatial biology research across multiple disease domains.
